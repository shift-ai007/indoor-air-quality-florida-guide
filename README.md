# Indoor Air Quality Monitoring: A Practical Guide for South Florida Homeowners

> Understanding what's in the air you breathe — and what your HVAC system should be doing about it.

## Why Indoor Air Quality Matters More in South Florida

South Florida's subtropical climate creates a unique indoor air quality challenge that most of the country doesn't face. With outdoor humidity regularly exceeding 80% and temperatures that keep homes sealed up with AC running for 8-10 months of the year, the air inside your home recirculates constantly through your HVAC system. If that system isn't properly maintained, it becomes a distribution network for pollutants instead of a filter for them.

The EPA estimates that indoor air can be 2-5 times more polluted than outdoor air. In South Florida, where homes stay closed up most of the year, that multiplier can be even higher.

## What to Monitor: The Key Indoor Air Quality Metrics

### Relative Humidity (Target: 45-50%)

This is the single most important metric for South Florida homes. Above 55%, dust mites multiply rapidly and mold spores begin germinating. Below 40%, you get dry skin and static electricity — rare in Florida but possible with an oversized dehumidifier.

**How to measure**: A basic hygrometer costs under $20. Place one in your main living area and one near a supply vent. If the readings differ by more than 10%, you likely have ductwork or insulation issues.

**What to do if it's high**: First, check that your AC is running proper cycle times (at least 10-15 minutes per cycle). Short-cycling units cool the air but don't dehumidify it. If cycle times are normal but humidity stays above 55%, your system may need [professional maintenance](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/) — a dirty evaporator coil is the most common culprit, losing up to 30% of its dehumidification capacity when coated with dust and biological growth.

### Particulate Matter (PM2.5 and PM10)

Fine particles from cooking, cleaning products, pet dander, and outdoor pollution that enters through the HVAC system. PM2.5 (particles under 2.5 microns) are especially dangerous because they penetrate deep into lung tissue.

**How to measure**: Consumer air quality monitors like the Purpleair or Awair cost $100-200 and give real-time readings. Anything above 12 µg/m³ for PM2.5 is worth investigating.

**What to do if it's high**: Upgrade your HVAC filter to MERV 11-13. Don't go higher without checking that your system can handle the pressure drop — a filter that's too restrictive starves your AC of airflow and causes more problems than it solves. If high particulate levels persist, consider [professional duct cleaning](https://ac-repair.today/services/duct-cleaning/) to remove accumulated debris that's being recirculated.

### Carbon Dioxide (CO2)

Elevated CO2 indicates poor ventilation — your home isn't getting enough fresh air exchange. Normal outdoor levels are around 400 ppm. Indoor levels above 1000 ppm cause drowsiness and reduced cognitive function.

**How to measure**: Most consumer IAQ monitors include CO2 sensors. Check readings during peak occupancy (everyone home in the evening).

**What to do if it's high**: This is a ventilation problem. Options include an ERV (Energy Recovery Ventilator) that brings in fresh air while recovering cooling energy, or simply running bathroom exhaust fans for 15-20 minutes several times a day.

### VOCs (Volatile Organic Compounds)

Off-gassing from furniture, paint, cleaning products, and building materials. Chronic exposure is linked to headaches, fatigue, and respiratory irritation.

**How to measure**: Consumer IAQ monitors report total VOC (tVOC) levels. Readings above 500 ppb warrant attention.

**What to do if it's high**: Increase ventilation when using cleaning products or after bringing new furniture home. Activated carbon filters in your HVAC system help, but the real fix is source control — switch to low-VOC products where possible.

## The HVAC Connection: Your System as Air Quality Infrastructure

Your air conditioning system isn't just about temperature — it's the primary [indoor air quality infrastructure](https://ac-repair.today/services/indoor-air-quality/) in your home. Every breath you take indoors has passed through your HVAC system. Here's how each component affects air quality:

### The Filter
Changes everything. A MERV 8 filter catches pollen and dust but misses mold spores, bacteria, and fine particulates. MERV 11-13 catches most airborne allergens. The key is changing it every 60-90 days in South Florida (more often during peak pollen season in spring).

### The Evaporator Coil
Dehumidifies the air and traps biological growth if not cleaned annually. A coil covered in biofilm doesn't just reduce efficiency — it actively releases particles into your airstream.

### The Ductwork
Old or poorly sealed ductwork in a hot attic pulls in unconditioned, humid air laden with insulation fibers and dust. Duct leakage can account for 30% of your total cooling load and introduce significant contaminants.

### The Drain Pan and Condensate Line
Standing water in a clogged drain pan is a mold factory. If your drain line backs up, that mold gets distributed through your entire duct system within days.

## When DIY Monitoring Isn't Enough

Consumer monitors give you trends, but they don't replace a professional assessment. Call a professional when:

- **Humidity stays above 55% despite AC running normally** — could indicate an oversized system, refrigerant charge issue, or duct problems
- **Anyone in the household develops new respiratory symptoms** — asthma, chronic cough, or allergic reactions that improve when away from home
- **You see any visible mold** near vents, in bathrooms, or on walls — visible growth means the problem is already advanced
- **Musty odors from the vents** — mold or mildew inside the air handler or ductwork
- **Your system is over 10 years old and has never had a comprehensive [commercial-grade HVAC assessment](https://ac-repair.today/services/commercial-hvac/)** — residential systems in South Florida degrade faster than national averages due to the constant humidity and salt air

## Seasonal Calendar: What to Monitor When

| Month | Priority | Action |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Jan-Feb | Low humidity risk | Good time for annual duct inspection |
| Mar-Apr | Pollen season | Change filters monthly, monitor PM2.5 |
| May-Jun | Humidity ramp-up | Check humidity daily, verify AC cycle times |
| Jul-Sep | Peak humidity + hurricane season | Maximum vigilance on humidity, check drain lines monthly |
| Oct-Nov | Transition period | Annual coil cleaning, pre-season maintenance |
| Dec | Lowest humidity | Baseline your IAQ readings for comparison |
